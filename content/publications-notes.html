GREENER principles for environmentally sustainable computational science

L. Lannelongue, H.-E. G. Aronson, A. Bateman, E. Birney, T. Caplan, M. Juckes, J. McEntyre, A. D. Morris, G. Reilly and M. Inouye, | Nat Comput Sci, vol. 3, no. 6, pp. 514–521, Jun. 2023, https://doi.org/10.1038/s43588-023-00461-y

GREENER (Governance, Responsibility, Estimation, Energy and embodied impacts, New collaborations, Education and Research)

Governance and responsibility individuals and institutions are both responsible. Ideas:

  • initiatives led by grad students and ECRs

  • IT teams in HPC centers –> provide training and information (logs)

  • PI –> awareness

Estimate and report the energy consumption of algorithms

Monitoring carbon footprint is essential to identify inefficiencies and opportunities for improvement. Not only individual/local work also in HPC and at the project level.

Requires a lot of information not always available like thermal design power, PUE.

Tackling energy and embodied impacts through new collaborations

Carbon intensity depends on location. Changing carbon intensity at the city/state level is impossible, moving research somewhere else would be also hard. Collaborating with people in low carbon intensity region may open new opportunities.

Water usage, ecological impacts, and carbon footprint from manufacturing hardware are also significant.

Data storage is key. Some estimate 10kgCO2e per 1TB of data a year. It get worse with duplicated data.

The importance of dedicated education and research efforts for ESCS (environmen-tally sustainable computational science)

Education is important to raise awareness.

  • Formal training at undergraduate programs

  • programminglanguages in research, such as R and Python 95, tend to be the leastenergy-efficient ones –> need for RSEs to help write efficient code.

Cultural change

People wait for technology to change instead of changing the way they use it.

Greencomputing and FAIR practices may both stimulate innovation andreduce financial costs.

Ten simple rules to make your computing more environmentally sustainable

Lannelongue L, Grealey J, Bateman A, Inouye M | PLoS Comput Biol 17(9): e1009324. https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pcbi.1009324

Rule 1: Calculate the carbon footprint of your work

The environmental impact of computing can be broadly divided between (i) the carbon footprint of powering the computers during the task itself; (ii) the impact of long-term data storage; and (iii) the life cycle footprint of the hardware.

servers represents 50% of data centre’s energy usage. Data storage represent 10%. end to end environmental impact is hard to calculate.

Rule 2: Include the carbon footprint in your cost–benefit analysis

Considere carbon footprint when deciding if a project is a good idea.

Rule 3: Keep, repair, and reuse devices to minimise electronic waste

have less devices, use them for as long as possible. Recycle correctly.

Rule 4: Choose your computing facility

Could computing?

Rule 5: Choose your hardware carefully

Allocating extra gigabytes to a task “just to be safe” or to avoid having to optimise the code increases the carbon footprint substantially

Rule 6: Increase efficiency of the code

  • Limit peak memory requirements

  • be wary of hardware choices resulting in only small runtime improvements

  • use recent optimised libraries

  • use profilers to optimise code

Rule 7: Be a frugal analyst

  • test and debug the entire pipeline on small example datasets

  • checkpoint your code (i.e., save intermediary states to resume from in case of error) to limit unnecessary computations.

Rule 8: Releasing a new software? Make its hardware requirements and carbon footprint clear

developers should make it clear what the best hardware choices are for each situation. Also memory allocation (it’s usually overestimated)

Rule 9: Be aware of unanticipated consequences of improved software efficiency

increased efficiency in a process leads to greater increases in usage

Rule 10: Offset your carbon footprint

Carbon offsetting is complex, with debated benefits, and should never replace decreasing GHG emissions in the first place; however, it can mitigate the environmental impact of a project.
